Real Life Examples

These are some of the many real life examples and studies of the negative effects of social media on teen mental health.

  • An 18-year-old from Dallas, Texas, began to spend an increasing amount of time scrolling through social media feeds on her phone, to the point where she stopped engaging in other activities like reading, listening to music, and spending time with friends. Unfortunately, this behavior is becoming increasingly common among young adults, with 45% of teenagers acknowledging being online "almost constantly," according to a study conducted by Pew Research. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) reports a significant increase in suicide rates for young adults over the past two decades, with females aged 15-24 experiencing an 87 percent increase and males in the same age group seeing a 30 percent increase in suicide rates. Disturbingly, social media use has been linked to suicidal thoughts and body image issues in teenagers. A study conducted by Facebook revealed that one in three teenage girls feel that their body image issues are worsened by Instagram, and 6 percent of teenagers in the United States who reported experiencing suicidal thoughts attributed them to their use of Instagram. The "fear of missing out" (fomo) phenomenon drives young people to continuously enhance their social media presence, leading them to take risks that may lead to uncomfortable situations or even cyberbullying. Teenagers who spend three to six hours or more online are at risk of interfering with important developmental activities such as spending time with peers or participating in school and extracurricular activities.
